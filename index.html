Teresa Teng

Asia's Eternal Queen of Pop

1958 - 1995

Who was Teresa Teng?

Teresa Teng (29 January 1953 – 8 May 1995), also known as Deng Lijun, was a Taiwanese singer, actress, musician and philanthropist. Referred as "Asia's eternal queen of pop," Teng became a cultural icon for her contributions to Mandopop, giving birth to the phrase, "Wherever there are Chinese people, there is the music of Teresa Teng.” [1]

Let's go back to the start.

Humble beginnings

Born to a poor family, Teng's talent for music was discovered at the early age of three. Her father's friend, an erhu or "Chinese two-stringed violin" player, became Teng’s first voice teacher and fostered her passion for music.

Teng's perfect pitch and her amiable demeanor won the hearts of judges in various singing competitions. Soon after winning her first major prize in 1964, she was able to support her family by singing and quit school to sing professionally. [1]

A star is born

In her early career, Teng appeared as a television host and actor. After her recording of a movie soundtrack gained attention, she signed a record contact and performed throughout Taiwan.

Teng was a transnational icon shortly after. Performing in Mandarin, Cantonese, Japanese, and English, she won legions of fans even in far-flung regions like Malasia, Singapore, and Indonesia. [1] [3]

A voice for action

In the early 1980s, political tension between mainland China and Taiwan led to her music being banned for some years in mainland China. Despite the ban, her music continued to play everywhere.

Her popularity was so great that it was said to rival that of Deng Xiaoping, then China’s paramount leader, with whom she shared a surname. A popular saying was that “Old Deng ruled the day and Little Teng the night.”

On May 27, 1989, Teng performed in “Singing Democratic Songs for China”, a concert held in Hong Kong. With over 300,000 people in the audience, it was considered to be one of the reasons that led to the events in Tiananmen Square later in June of the same year. [1] [3] [4]

Teng's Legacy

After Teng’s sudden death in 1995, her albums, CDs, videos, and cassettes sold out across East Asia. Her funeral became the largest scale state-sponsored funeral there since ROC leader Chiang Kaishek’s funeral in 1975, with over 60,000 mourners spaning 30 miles.

To the Chinese who grew up with her music, Teng was way more than just a pop singer. She became a symbol of unity between mainland China, Taiwan and Hong Kong in the 1970s and 1980s when politics were pushing the three of them apart. Suija Yao states that “[Teng] is an exception that can unify Greater China, even Pan-Asia, without any military power or political enforcement”

Even almost 30 years after her death, Teng’s legacy lives on as a inter-generational cultural force in the modern Chinese diaspora. Teng continues to be widely admired in China and pan-Asia as “the forever Deng Lijun.” [6][7]
